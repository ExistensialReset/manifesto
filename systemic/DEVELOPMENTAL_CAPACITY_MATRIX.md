1.1 The Tension ‚öñÔ∏è

Two truths that seem to conflict:

1. Children have absolute veto over AI use affecting them (CHILDRENS_VOICE_AND_VETO.md)


2. Children cannot always foresee consequences of their choices



Resolution: Veto is always respected, but how we respond to it varies by:

Child's developmental stage

Type of decision

Reversibility of consequences

Harm potential


1.2 What This Is NOT ‚ùå

Permission to override children "for their own good"

Justification for adult authoritarianism

Loophole to ignore children's preferences


This IS ‚úÖ:

Framework for dialogue when veto creates serious harm risk

Recognition that capacity varies by age and individual

Commitment to always take children's concerns seriously

2. DEVELOPMENTAL STAGES (Guidelines, Not Rules) üë∂üßíüßëüßë‚Äçü¶±

2.1 Ages 0-5: Protected Innocence üçº

Cognitive Capacity: Pre-operational thinking (Piaget), concrete and immediate concerns, limited future orientation, high emotional reasoning

Veto Status: Guardians decide

Children this young cannot meaningfully consent to or refuse AI

Guardians make choices on their behalf

Default: Minimal AI exposure


AI Interaction:

Only if medically necessary

Guardian-supervised educational tools (if used at all)

No data collection beyond medical necessity


Example Scenario: 4-year-old refuses AI-assisted insulin pump

Guardian explains simply: "This helps your body stay healthy"

If child still refuses: manual insulin management

Re-introduce gradually with play-based familiarization

Never force against sustained refusal


2.2 Ages 6-9: Emerging Agency üß∏

Cognitive Capacity: Concrete operational thinking, developing cause-effect, still present-focused, beginning moral reasoning

Veto Status: Respected with dialogue

Child's veto honored

Adults provide age-appropriate info

Reversible decisions: child decides

Irreversible decisions: extended dialogue + re-evaluation


AI Interaction: Observer in Mirror meetings, storytelling, supervised hands-on experiments, can refuse with no consequences

Example Scenario: 7-year-old refuses AI classroom tools

Immediate veto respected

Dialogue: "What worries you about the AI?"

Provide AI-free alternatives, revisit quarterly

No pressure


2.3 Ages 10-13: Developing Abstract Thought üßë‚Äçüéì

Cognitive Capacity: Early formal operational thinking, imagines future consequences, peer influence increases, identity formation begins

Veto Status: Strong presumption of validity

Veto presumed informed unless proven otherwise

Adults provide detailed info

Youth involved in how veto is implemented

Irreversible harm: structured dialogue before final decision


Example Scenario: 12-year-old refuses AI tutoring

Immediate veto respected

Dialogue: "What would help you learn better?"

Alternative: human tutor, peer study group

Check-in: outcome matters, not method

No forced AI use


2.4 Ages 14-17: Near-Adult Capacity üßë‚Äçüíº

Cognitive Capacity: Formal operational thinking, abstract reasoning, identity more stable, capable of long-term planning

Veto Status: Presumed fully informed

Treated as adult decision unless serious concern

Youth participates in consequences of choice

Irreversible harm: ethics council review


Example Scenario: 16-year-old refuses AI in college applications

Veto respected, manual apps only

Consequences explained, youth choice maintained

Fully valid decision

Three Variables:

1. Reversibility

3. Child's Understanding



Matrix:

Reversibility	Harm Potential	Age	Protocol

4. LIFE-THREATENING SCENARIOS üíî

Scenario: Child refuses life-saving AI (e.g., glucose monitor)

Process:

1. Exhaust alternatives (manual, mentorship, support) ‚Äì 2 weeks


2. Family meeting (child, guardians, doctor, trusted adult) ‚Äì understand fears, explain consequences


3. Ethics Council (if unresolved) ‚Äì 72h window, includes youth advocate, child present


4. Council recommendation (not decree): Veto stands / Temporary trial / Compromise / Override (last resort, <14yo, time-limited, monthly review)



Post-Override Protocol: psychological support, transparency, re-evaluation, apology

5. SPECIAL POPULATIONS üåà

Neurodivergent children: Capacity varies, use preferred communication, veto valid

Deaf/Blind children: Accessible info, veto applies equally

Traumatized children: Extra time to build trust, veto may remain absolute for years



---

6. CULTURAL VARIATIONS üåç

Framework uses Western developmental models, but adapt to culture

Indigenous knowledge: involve elders, ceremony, early autonomy



---

7. MONITORING & ACCOUNTABILITY üîç

Quarterly review: veto occurrences, overrides, outcomes

Annual audit: external advocates, surveys with 14+ children

Red flags: frequent overrides, children feeling unheard

8. TRAINING MATERIALS üéì

9. EDGE CASES & OPEN QUESTIONS ‚ùì

Child refuses life-saving AI, death certain without it

Child's veto harms siblings

Child changes mind repeatedly

Framework evolves with real-world experience, philosophy, child input

Sunset clause: review every 3 years

10. SUMMARY PRINCIPLES ‚úÖ

1. Veto is presumed valid at all ages


2. Dialogue is information-sharing, not persuasion


3. Override is trauma, almost never occur


4. Protection from harm ‚â† protection from choice


5. Adults' discomfort is not children's problem


6. When in doubt, honor refusal

11. COMMITMENT STATEMENT ‚úä
