## What Refugium Anima Is

Refugium Anima is a **physical and relational space** designed for people who have temporarily lost access to Flow.

Not because they are broken.  
Not because they are ill.  
But because **life sometimes overwhelms the nervous system** faster than it can recover on its own.

This is not a hospital.  
This is not a treatment facility.  
This is not a place of diagnosis, correction, or rehabilitation.

**Refugium Anima is sanctuary.**

PREAMBLE: Why Children Need Refugium Anima

Children are not small adults. Their nervous systems are still forming, their boundaries are still being learned, and their voices are not always heard. When a child is overwhelmed—by trauma, by sensory overload, by grief, by the sheer weight of being alive in a world not built for them—they rarely have words for it. They act it out. They scream. They hit. They hide. They freeze.

Refugium Anima does not punish these responses. It welcomes them.

This document extends the original Refugium Anima protocol to explicitly include children—not as a separate wing, not as a “child-friendly” version, but as the same sanctuary, designed to hold all bodies, all ages, all nervous systems.

Here, a child and an adult may share the same space, or they may not. The choice belongs to the one who needs rest.

1. CORE PRINCIPLES (Expanded for All Ages)

1.1 No Performance Expected

· No one, regardless of age, must justify their presence.
· No progress tracking, no “therapeutic goals,” no milestones to reach.
· Being here is enough.

1.2 No Productivity Required

· No tasks, schedules, or goals unless requested by the individual.
· The body sets the pace—whether that body is 4 years old or 84 years old.
· Play is not “therapeutic” here; it is simply allowed.

1.3 No Justification Demanded

· Silence is honored.
· Sharing is optional.
· A child’s tears are not interrogated. An adult’s rage is not pathologized.

1.4 Collapse is Welcome

· Rest, breakdown, screaming, hitting (in safe spaces), and non-engagement are accepted without judgment.
· Regression is not failure; it is part of healing.

1.5 Animals and Nature

· Access to animals, gardens, and outdoor spaces is essential for all ages.
· Non-human presence supports regulation without demand.

1.6 Consent is Absolute

· For children, consent means age-appropriate understanding and the right to say no.
· Adults may never override a child’s refusal to participate in any activity, including being spoken to.

2. THE SHARED SPACES: What Exists Inside Refugium Anima

All spaces are designed for all bodies—wheelchair accessible, sensory-aware, and free of clinical or infantilizing aesthetics. Children and adults share these spaces, but the spaces are designed so that individuals can also be alone.

2.1 Rest & Regulation Spaces

· Adjustable lighting (dim to bright, warm to cool) and sound (silence to gentle white noise).
· Beds, mats, hammocks, weighted blankets at various sizes (child and adult).
· Temperature control; accessible design throughout.
· Child adaptation: Low beds, soft floor cushions, small cozy nooks for hiding.

2.2 The Scream & Impact Room (Ljudisolerat Skrikrum)

· A fully soundproofed room where sound and impact are allowed.
· Walls padded with safe, durable material.
· Boxing ball (boxboll) suspended from the ceiling, adjustable in height so a child can reach it.
· Soft floor mats for falling, rolling, or lying down.
· No observation windows. No cameras. What happens here stays here.
· Child adaptation: The ball can be lowered; soft bats or pillows available; the room is not “scary” but welcoming—painted in calming colors, with optional dim lighting.

2.3 Water & Body Relief (The Bath Room)

· Warm baths and soaking areas with adjustable water temperature.
· Ceiling lift or mobile hoist so a disabled person (like Elinor) can safely enter and exit the bath.
· Handrails, non-slip flooring, wide doorways.
· Soft towels, bath salts, and privacy.
· A place to weep, to soak, to let the water hold you.
· Child adaptation: Small bath seats, floating toys available but never required; parent or witness may assist only if invited.

2.4 The Unfinished Studio (Creative & Play Space)

· Raw materials with no instructions, goals, or cameras: clay, paint, paper, fabric, wood, sound-making objects.
· Tools accessible to all abilities—adaptive grips, low tables, voice-activated options.
· The “No-Audience Rule”: Creations belong solely to the individual unless voluntarily shared.
· Child adaptation: Everything is at child height; materials are non-toxic; there is space to make a mess without being “cleaned up” by adults.

2.5 The Sensory Garden

· A walled or hidden outdoor space with plants that respond to touch (lambs ear, soft grasses).
· Wind chimes, water features, places to sit or lie on the ground.
· Edible plants (berries, herbs) that can be picked and eaten without permission.
· Accessible paths for wheelchairs and walkers.
· Child adaptation: Low-growing plants, a small digging patch, hiding spots.

2.6 The Silence Void

· A central open space dedicated to calm and reflection.
· No talking. No eye contact required. Simply being together or alone in shared presence.
· Soft lighting, cushions, and a view of the garden.
· Child adaptation: Children may be present, but if they cannot be silent, they are gently guided to another space—without shame.

3. CHILD-SPECIFIC ADAPTATIONS (Built In, Not Bolted On)

3.1 Age-Appropriate Agency

· Children may use any space, at any time, with or without an adult, provided safety is maintained.
· A child may say “no” to any offer of company, activity, or food.
· A child may request a specific witness (adult) to be present or absent.

3.2 No Interpretation Without Consent

· Adults (including Witnesses) do not interpret a child’s behavior to the child.
· No “You’re angry because…” or “You’re sad because…” unless the child asks.
· Feelings are witnessed, not diagnosed.

3.3 The Right to Be Incomprehensible

· A child may be incoherent, contradictory, or silent.
· They do not owe anyone an explanation.

3.4 Protection from Adult Pain

· Children are never used as emotional support for adults in Refugium.
· Adult tears, rage, or collapse are witnessed by other adults, not by children.
· If a child witnesses an adult’s distress, a child-specific witness explains in age-appropriate terms and ensures the child feels safe.

3.5 The Child’s Own Witness

· Every child in Refugium has access to a designated child-witness—an adult trained in child development, trauma, and non-verbal communication.
· This witness is chosen by the child (if possible) and may be changed at any time.
· The witness advocates for the child’s needs to the Circle without burdening the child.

---

4. ACCESSIBILITY FOR ALL BODIES

Refugium Anima is designed from the ground up for all bodies:

· Wheelchair-accessible paths, doors, and bathrooms.
· Adjustable-height furniture and fixtures.
· Sensory accommodations for neurodivergent individuals (quiet hours, dim lighting options, fidget objects available).
· Medical equipment (lift, adjustable bed) available without stigma.
· No one is too heavy, too disabled, too young, or too old to be here.

---

5. THE ROLE OF WITNESSES (Including for Children)

Witnesses are not therapists. They are human anchors—trained to be present without demand.

5.1 General Witness Principles

· Presence Without Demand: “I am here, and the space is held.”
· Non-Invasive Proximity: Physical and energetic distance respects hypervigilance.
· Reflective Sovereignty: “In this space, your will is the only law.”

5.2 Witnesses for Children

· Child witnesses are trained in child development, play therapy (non-directive), and trauma-informed care.
· They do not “play with” the child unless invited.
· They do not interpret or report on the child’s behavior to the Circle without the child’s consent (age-appropriate).
· They act as a shield between the child and any legal or systemic processes.

5.3 The Silent Exit

· Any individual—child or adult—may end an interaction at any time by simply closing their eyes, turning away, or leaving.
· Witness departs immediately, without a “goodbye” that requires a social response.
