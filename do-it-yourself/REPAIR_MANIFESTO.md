## PART I: THE PSYCHOLOGY OF REPAIR

### Why We Don't Repair (And How to Start Anyway)

**"I'm not good enough"**

**The lie:** Only experts can fix things.

**The truth:** Most repairs are simple. You just haven't tried yet. Skills build through trial and error, fostering resilience.

**The antidote:** Start with something broken that you don't care about. If you break it more, nothing is lost. If you fix it ‚Äì you learned. Track your progress in a repair journal to build confidence over time.

---

**"I don't have tools"**

**The lie:** You need expensive tools to repair.

**The truth:** Most basic repairs need: screwdriver, pliers, duct tape, glue. Many communities offer free access.

**The antidote:** Borrow tools. Join tool library. Buy one tool at a time when needed. Consider second-hand markets or online swaps to avoid feeding new production.

---

**"It's faster to buy new"**

**The lie:** Time is money.

**The truth:** Time spent learning is investment. Next time it's faster. Plus, the environmental cost of "new" far outweighs your time.

**The antidote:** Repair is meditation. Slow down. Enjoy the process. Frame it as a deliberate act of Flow‚Äîwhere spontaneity meets focus, leading to deeper satisfaction.

---

**"New is better"**

**The lie:** New things are higher quality.

**The truth:** Old things were often built better. New things are designed to break. Repairing preserves quality and reduces e-waste.

**The antidote:** See the beauty in worn things. Scars tell stories. Embrace wabi-sabi: the aesthetic of impermanence and imperfection.

---

**"It's not safe"**

**The lie:** Repairing could be dangerous, especially with electronics or machinery.

**The truth:** With basic precautions, most repairs are safe. Ignorance breeds fear, but knowledge empowers.

**The antidote:** Always unplug devices before working. Wear safety gear like gloves and goggles. Research hazards first‚Äîmany guides include safety notes. If unsure, consult a pro, but use it as a learning opportunity.

---

**"I don't know where to start"**

**The lie:** You need a formal course or certification to begin.

**The truth:** Every expert started as a beginner. The first step is simply to look closely at the broken item and ask, "What's wrong?"

**The antidote:** Adopt the "Five-Minute Rule": Commit to examining the broken item for just five minutes. Often, the problem reveals itself, and the next step becomes clear. Use the "One Thing" principle from the Existential Reset‚Äîfocus on the single, smallest actionable step.

## PART VII: WHEN REPAIR ISN'T POSSIBLE

Sometimes things are truly dead.

That's okay.

But before giving up:

**Ask:**

1. Can this be parts for something else?
2. Can someone else use this (even broken)?
3. Can this be raw material (wood, metal, fabric)?
4. Is there a modular upgrade path?
5. Can it be artistically transformed?

**Disposal hierarchy:**

1. Repair
2. Repurpose / Upcycle
3. Cannibalize for parts
4. Give away / Donate for parts
5. Recycle properly
6. Trash (last resort)

**Eco-Check:** Research local e-waste programs to ensure responsible recycling, avoiding export to harmful dumps. Use databases like ewasteguide.info or recyclenow.com.

**Post-Mortem Analysis:** Conduct a "failure autopsy." Why did it die? Was it planned obsolescence? A cheap part? Document the cause to inform future purchases and advocacy.

## PART X: REPAIR AND THE CLIMATE ‚Äì THE NUMBERS THAT MATTER

Every repair is a direct climate action. Here‚Äôs how:

**Carbon Saved:**
* Extending a laptop's life by 3 years avoids ~150kg CO2e (equivalent to a car trip from Stockholm to Copenhagen).
* Repairing and reusing a washing machine saves ~50% of the carbon footprint compared to buying new.
* The global repair movement could reduce e-waste by millions of tonnes annually‚Äîe-waste is a major source of toxic leakage and greenhouse gases when incinerated.

**Resource Conservation:**
* One repaired smartphone saves ~13,000 liters of water (used in mining and manufacturing).
* Mending clothes reduces demand for fast fashion, which accounts for 10% of global carbon emissions.

**Biodiversity Impact:**
* Reducing demand for new goods lessens deforestation, mining, and habitat destruction.
* Fewer products in landfills means less soil and water contamination.

**Actionable Climate Steps:**
1. **Calculate Your Repair Footprint:** Use online calculators to estimate CO2 saved per repair.
2. **Advocate for Repair-Friendly Policies:** Support legislation that mandates repairability indexes (like in France) and holds companies accountable for full life-cycle impact.
3. **Join the Circular Economy:** Prioritize buying repaired, refurbished, or repairable items. Support local repair businesses.
4. **Share the Data:** When you post a repair, include the estimated resources saved. Make the impact visible.

Remember: The most sustainable product is the one that already exists. Keeping it alive is a radical act of ecological defense.
