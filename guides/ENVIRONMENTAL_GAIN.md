## Verified Environmental Impacts of the Current System

**Food Waste** ‚Äì FAO (2022): ~30% of all produced food is lost or wasted (13.2% in the supply chain, 17% at consumer/retail level). This represents a major resource and climate burden.

**Fossil Fuel Subsidies** ‚Äì IMF (2023): $7 trillion in global subsidies (direct + indirect). These subsidies slow the transition to renewable energy and perpetuate carbon-intensive infrastructure.

**Planned Obsolescence** ‚Äì EU Parliament (2020): Confirmed as a widespread phenomenon. While reuse and recycling help, the total energy saved is often smaller than expected, contributing to resource depletion and growing waste streams, particularly in electronics.

**Renewable Energy Potential** ‚Äì IRENA (2023): Technical potential for solar and wind far exceeds global demand. IEA (2023): Renewable energy is now often the cheapest option for electricity generation. The primary barriers are political, financial, and infrastructural, not technical.

## Science vs. Manifesto Claims

- **Where the manifesto is accurate:** Problem scale (30% food waste, $7T subsidies) aligns well with official data. The idea that technical resources exist for a sustainable society is supported by research.  
- **Where the manifesto overestimates:** Its upper-bound gains (25‚Äì70% reductions) assume perfect, frictionless global implementation. Real-world change is slower and complex. Achieving a 70% reduction in agriculture's environmental impact would also require a global shift to plant-based diets and revolutionary farming methods‚Äîdecades-long efforts.

## Potential Gains for Earth

If the world aggressively implemented scientifically backed measures within a **reformed market framework**, potential gains over 20‚Äì30 years include:

1. **Climate:** ~30% lower global CO2 emissions, mainly from phasing out coal and fossil fuel subsidies.  
2. **Resources:** ~20‚Äì30% less waste from reduced food waste and planned obsolescence.  
3. **Economy:** $7 trillion/year freed from fossil subsidies for investment in health, education, and green infrastructure.

**Conclusion:** Maximum environmental impact comes from tackling verifiable inefficiencies (food waste, subsidies) rather than implementing a fully new global system. The manifesto provides inspiration, but real data from FAO, IMF, and IEA show measurable gains.
